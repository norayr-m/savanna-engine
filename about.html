SAVANNA ENGINE

A living digital Serengeti — 1 million nodes, 61 frames per second

For Everyone

You're watching a million tiny patches of African grassland, alive on your screen. Green is grass. Dark patches are where zebra herds have eaten everything bare. Blue pools are watering holes. White dots are zebras. Red dots are lions.

The zebras travel in herds, eating grass as they go. When they've stripped an area, they move on — the grass slowly grows back behind them. Every few days they must visit a watering hole or they'll weaken.

Lions are solitary hunters. They wait near water, because every animal must drink. When a zebra comes too close, the lion charges — covering two hexes in a single explosive burst. If it connects, the zebra dies and the lion feeds for weeks.

Pinch the screen to zoom. As you zoom in, time slows down — you can watch a single hunt unfold. Zoom out and time speeds up — you watch seasons pass, populations rise and fall.

For the Curious

This is a Lotka-Volterra predator-prey system, but spatial — not just equations on a chalkboard. Each animal is an autonomous agent with three senses (hearing, sight, smell) and a facing direction. Herding, hunting, and migration all emerge from local rules. Nobody tells the zebras to form herds — they align their movement with neighbors (Vicsek flocking model) and it happens.

Every animal has a ternary metabolic state: active (+1), resting (0), or refractory (-1). Active costs energy. Resting conserves. For zebras, refractory means panic (burns double). For lions, it means torpor — a shutdown mode that costs nothing. A starving lion drifts like a ghost across the savanna, burning zero energy, until it smells prey.

The zoom isn't just visual — it's spacetime zoom. Time scales with the square of the spatial zoom: t ~ 1/x^1.5`. This is Lévy superdiffusion scaling, the same law that governs how temperature diffuses through metal. At the microscale, you watch individual cells. At the macroscale, you watch population waves. Same physics, different resolution.

Water creates metapopulation structure. Each of the 12 lakes is a habitat patch. Animals cluster near water, creating high-density zones connected by low-density grassland corridors. The ecosystem isn't one big soup — it's a network of oases.

For Engineers

The state tensor is [entity, energy, ternary, gauge, orientation] × 1,048,576 nodes, updated via 7 Metal compute shader dispatches per tick. The hex grid uses 7-coloring: (col + row + 4*(col&1)) % 7, verified by exhaustive search to have zero distance-1 AND distance-2 conflicts. This is the chromatic number for distance-2 independence on the hexagonal lattice (Molloy & Salavatipour 2005).

Each color group is an independent set — nodes in the same group share no common neighbor. This permits safe concurrent read-neighbor, write-self, AND write-to-neighbor (movement) without atomics or locks. The update is a chromatic Gauss-Seidel relaxation: each group sees the latest state from previously processed groups within the same tick.

Four scent diffusion fields (Float32) propagate information beyond direct neighbors. Zebra scent (decay 0.98) reaches ~50 hexes — lions track herds from far away. Water scent (decay 0.995) reaches ~200 hexes — a permanent gradient map that acts as spatial memory. Animals don't store "where is water" — they read the gradient.

The ternary firewall enforces 0 → +1 → -1 → 0. No direct -1 → +1 transition, except the predation interrupt: a starving lion that contacts prey jumps directly to active. Base metabolic rate is per-species, per-state, per-tick — applied at the top of the kernel before any behavior logic, so no early-return path can bypass it.

The unsolved problem: stable Lotka-Volterra limit cycles have not emerged. The system produces boom-bust (lions overhunt, go extinct, zebras recover) but not oscillation. The spatial Rosenzweig-MacArthur model with discrete hex topology may require a Type II functional response (predator satiation) to stabilize. This is an open research question.
